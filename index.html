Kit 1 · Perception — how machines sense the world

Computers perceive the world through sensors — converting physical signals (sound, light, motion) into data an algorithm can process. This kit builds an accessibility sensor, analyzes images as pixel grids, and connects voice-assistant phonics to AI hearing.

SensingSignal processingNoise filteringThresholdingFeature extraction
Core tools
Speech spectrogram
Visualize voice as frequency data — exactly how AI "hears" before processing.
spectrogram.sciencemusic.org
ML for Kids — sound classifier
Record 20+ samples/class (alarm, voice, silence). Train. Trigger a Scratch visual alert.
machinelearningforkids.co.uk
MIT App Inventor
Package sound detection as a real Android vibration-alert app.
appinventor.mit.edu
Build steps — Lesson 3.2

Design & create

  • 1Pick a learning space. Use the Empathize Template: target action → data type → sensor → AI perception needed.
  • 2Record 20+ audio samples per class in ML for Kids. Label carefully — this is your training data.
  • 3Train. Note confidence. Add samples where accuracy is below 80%. Retrain and compare.
  • 4In Scratch, trigger a visual flash when the model detects an alert sound.
  • 5Extend to App Inventor: package the model as a real phone notification app.

Reflect

  • R1Test on 10 new sounds not in training. Track failures — what patterns do you see?
  • R2How might a noise-cancellation algorithm treat accents or non-English speech differently?
  • R3Your sensor works in a quiet room. How would you redesign it for a cafeteria?
Cross-disciplinary lessons
ScienceElem
5.1: Renewable energy & solar sensing
NGSS 4-PS3-2 · Energy transfer
Attach sensors to solar panels. AI processes sensor data to optimize orientation — the same sensing pipeline as your hearing sensor.
MathMiddle
6.1: Statistics in digital photography
CCSS 6.SP.B.5 · Distributions
Work with a 5×5 pixel grid (0–255). Calculate mean, median, mode — exactly how AI "sees" an image. Apply thresholding to convert to black-and-white.
ELAElem
7.2: Phonics — decoding complex words
CCSS RF.2.3.B · Vowel teams
Practice vowel teams with a voice assistant. Try to "trick" it with mispronunciations — reveals its perception limits. Parallels how AI tokenizes speech.

A camera represents an image as numbers; a mic represents sound as frequency data. What is always lost when the real world becomes data — and who decides what gets discarded?

Book's core question: How might a noise-cancellation algorithm treat accents or non-English speech? What does that reveal about whose voices AI is trained to hear?

Kit 2 · Representation & reasoning — how machines think

Computers structure knowledge as data and use reasoning algorithms to make decisions. This kit builds a Netflix recommender chatbot, uses probability trees to model weather-prediction AI, and connects literary annotation to AI feature extraction.

Collaborative filteringContent-based filteringConditional probabilityFeature extractionSentiment analysis
Core tools
Scratch — recommender chatbot
Store user preferences in lists. Use if/else logic to match and suggest — collaborative or content-based filtering made tangible.
scratch.mit.edu
Desmos — scatter plots & similarity
Plot users in a 2D preference space to show nearest-neighbor filtering. Also used in Lesson 6.4 for regression.
teacher.desmos.com
GeoGebra — probability trees
Draw decision trees for Lesson 6.2 weather-prediction AI. Also tessellations as a training-dataset simulation.
geogebra.org
Build steps — Lesson 3.1

Design & create

  • 1Choose community and item type. Decide: collaborative (similar users) or content-based (similar features)?
  • 2Sketch the algorithm as a flowchart before coding — if user liked X and Y, suggest Z.
  • 3In Scratch: build lists of items and preferences. Wire "when [user picks X] → suggest [Y]" logic.
  • 4Add a chatbot sprite: 3 questions → personalized recommendation.
  • 5Test with 5 peers. Log wrong outputs. Is the failure in data, logic, or categories?

Reflect

  • R1Netflix reinforces what you already watch. How could you redesign your recommender to avoid filter bubbles?
  • R2What data would improve your recommender? What data would make it biased?
  • R3What does human recommendation use that your algorithm cannot model?
Cross-disciplinary lessons
ScienceMiddle
5.3: Natural selection simulation
NGSS MS-LS4-4 · Natural selection
Adjust predator/food in PhET sim; observe rabbit population. Parallels AI genetic algorithms: encoding traits as data, running selection pressure, optimizing on feedback.
MathHigh school
6.2: Probability trees & weather prediction
CCSS HSS-CP.A.2 · Conditional probability
70% cloudiness → 60% humidity if cloudy → 80% rain if humid. Build the probability tree and compute total rain probability — exactly how a Bayesian AI weather model works.
ELAMiddle
7.1: Analyzing character development
CCSS RL.6.3 · Plot and character
Annotate a character timeline (The Giver, Wonder, Percy Jackson). Each step maps to AI: key event = data selection; emotional arc = sentiment analysis; cause-effect = pattern recognition.

Your recommender, the probability tree, and the character timeline all represent knowledge differently. What does each make easy to compute — and what human nuance does each lose?

Book's core question: Netflix's algorithm can reinforce what you already watch. How could you redesign your recommender to ensure users occasionally encounter something genuinely unexpected?

Kit 3 · Learning — how machines learn from data

ML means computers find patterns in labeled data to make predictions. This kit trains a supervised ML model on climate images, parallels physics experiments with ML training, and shows how a word board game teaches the same logic as NLP data labeling.

Supervised learningNeural networksTraining & test setsData labelingLinear regression
Core tools
ML for Kids — image classifier
Label climate images (healthy/deforested/flooded). Train. Connect to Scratch alert when damage is detected.
machinelearningforkids.co.uk
Desmos — line of best fit
Collect height–shoe size data. Plot, fit manually, then compare to linear regression. The math core of supervised ML.
teacher.desmos.com
PhET — Build a Molecule + Natural Selection
Building molecules from atomic rules parallels supervised ML training. Natural Selection sim shows genetic algorithm learning.
phet.colorado.edu
Build steps — Lesson 3.3

Design & create

  • 1Use the Identity Wheel to pick an environmental issue connected to your community.
  • 2Collect and label 20+ images per class. Quality of labels = quality of model.
  • 3Train in ML for Kids. If any class is below 80%, add 10 samples and retrain.
  • 4Connect to Scratch: show alert + confidence score when damage is detected.
  • 5Test on 10 unseen images. Calculate real-world accuracy. Compare to training accuracy.

Reflect

  • R1Training vs. test accuracy gap — what does a large gap mean? This is "overfitting." Why does it matter for real-world AI deployment?
  • R2If your training images mostly showed one region's forests, what communities might be harmed by geographic bias?
  • R3Physics models are validated against new data (Lesson 5.5). You tested on 10 unseen images. Why does every discipline insist on validation with new data?
Cross-disciplinary lessons
ScienceHigh school
5.5: Conservation of momentum
NGSS HS-PS2-2 · Mathematical representations
Collect collision data with Vernier sensors or CK12 sim. Derive a mathematical model from data — parallels ML: labeled examples → predictions. Validation, iteration, and overfitting all apply.
MathHigh school
6.4: Line of best fit & regression
CCSS HSS-ID.B.6 · Scatter plots
Measure height + shoe size. Plot. Fit manually. Compare to calculator regression. Introduces training vs. test sets and asks: how does dataset size affect generalization?
ELAElem
7.4: Words with multiple meanings
CCSS L.4.4.A · Context clues
Board game: match multiple-meaning words to context sentences and picture cards — matching = data labeling, correct card = ground truth, win/lose = reinforcement signal.

In the board game, students are the data labelers. In your climate model, you labeled the training images. Who decides what "deforestation" looks like in the Philippines vs. Brazil — and how might those choices embed cultural assumptions?

Book's core question: If your training images mostly showed one region's forests, how would that limit the model — and who might it harm when deployed in a different geography?

Kit 4 · Natural interaction — how machines understand humans

Natural interaction means AI that communicates intuitively. This kit uses GenAI as a creative partner in fashion design, explores commonsense reasoning limits through Earth's geological history, and stages a structured debate on whether AI can ever truly understand human communication.

NLPGenerative AIGANsPrompt engineeringCommonsense reasoning
Core tools
ChatGPT — image generation & debate prep
Generate fashion designs (book shows: Renaissance dress, futuristic blazer, smart sneakers). Also used in Lesson 7.5 to generate debate counterarguments.
chatgpt.com
Canva AI — text-to-image
Create cultural holiday posters (Lesson 8.3) or fashion mood boards. Explicitly named in the book's social studies curriculum.
canva.com
EarthViewer (HHMI Biointeractive)
4.5 billion years of Earth history. Students reason about geological cause-and-effect — paralleling the challenge AI faces in commonsense reasoning (Lessons 5.6–5.7).
biointeractive.org
Build steps — Lesson 3.4

Design & create

  • 1Define your collection theme (3–5 garments). Research real design trends from one era before prompting.
  • 2Write 5 prompts for the same garment — vary fabric, mood, cultural reference. Compare outputs.
  • 3Identify which prompt elements changed the output most. Document as a design log.
  • 4Build a Scratch style-quiz chatbot: 3 questions → outfit description output.
  • 5Present: original prompt → output → refined prompt → final. Show design log to class.

Reflect

  • R1Which cultural aesthetics does GenAI default to? Which body types or cultures are absent from its outputs?
  • R2If a GAN trained primarily on European fashion photography, what would it know — and not know — about global fashion traditions?
  • R3Does ChatGPT "understand" what makes a design beautiful — or is it producing statistically likely pixel combinations? Is there a meaningful difference?
Cross-disciplinary lessons
ScienceElem/HS
5.6–5.7: Earth's features & Earth's age
NGSS 4-ESS2-2 / HS-ESS1-5
EarthViewer: reason about geological cause-effect across 4.5 billion years. The book notes AI has not yet achieved commonsense reasoning about ill-defined physical problems — students experience this firsthand.
ELAHigh school
7.5: Debating the future of AI
CCSS SL.11-12.1 · Collaborative discussion
Two teams debate: "AI can achieve true natural interaction" vs. "AI always lacks human depth." Use Poll Everywhere for opinion polls, ChatGPT for counterarguments, mind-map tools for argument structure.
Soc. studiesElem
8.3: Celebrating together — cultural holidays
CCSS D2.Civ.7.K-2 · Civic virtues
Create cultural holiday posters using Canva AI text-to-image. Students analyze what the AI includes and what it misrepresents — NLP + image generation working together.

Your GenAI fashion designs and Canva's holiday posters both take text and produce images. Cultural imagery is deeply meaningful — a mistake can be disrespectful. What responsibilities do AI designers have when systems interact across cultures?

Book's core question: How might biases in the training data influence which designs GenAI favors? Whose definition of "fashionable" is baked into the model — and who decided what images to train it on?

Kit 5 · Societal impact — how AI shapes the world

AI raises ethical and social questions: job displacement, algorithmic bias, privacy, accountability, and the digital divide. This kit builds an autonomous agent in Scratch, interrogates AI's role in genetic engineering, and analyzes how AI affects civic participation and access inequality.

Autonomous agentsAlgorithmic biasAI ethicsDigital divideCivic AI
Core tools
Scratch — autonomous search-and-rescue agent
Full step-by-step Scratch code in the book. Build a robot that navigates a maze, detects obstacles, and rescues a target — without human input.
scratch.mit.edu
MIT Moral Machine
Play ethical dilemmas. See global variation in decisions. Write 3 ethical rules for your agent — then code at least one.
moralmachine.net
ChatGPT — counterargument stress-test
Explicitly used in Lesson 7.5 for ethics debates. Prompt it to argue against your agent's design decisions — then revise in response to the best counterarguments.
chatgpt.com
Build steps — Lesson 3.5

Design & create

  • 1Scratch: new project, delete cat, build maze backdrop with obstacles and rescue target sprite.
  • 2Add robot sprite. Verify basic movement with arrow keys before automating.
  • 3Collision detection: touching obstacle → stop. Touching target → broadcast "rescued."
  • 4Make it autonomous: remove human controls. Add random movement + wall-avoidance loop.
  • 5Play Moral Machine. Write 3 ethical rules. Code at least one. Use ChatGPT to argue against your rules — revise if compelling.

Reflect

  • R1Your agent made a mistake and harmed a rescue target. Who is responsible — coder, deployer, or algorithm? How does the answer change depending on how much testing was done?
  • R2What tasks in a real rescue scenario should always require a human — and why? What is lost when we remove human judgment from high-stakes decisions?
  • R3If autonomous rescue agents were only deployed in wealthy neighborhoods due to infrastructure costs, how would that compound existing inequality?

Soc. studiesHigh school
8.5: Bridging the gap — AI & the digital divide
Economics · Marginal costs & benefits
Analyze NTIA, US Census, and Pew Research data on the Digital Divide. Calculate marginal costs and benefits of AI-powered educational tools — who gains access, and who is excluded?

Your rescue agent, an AI voter chatbot, and an AI genetic selection tool all act without direct human control at the moment of action. In each case: who should have veto power — and how would that veto actually work in practice?

Book's core question: If your rescue robot made a mistake that harmed someone, who is responsible — the coder, the deployer, or the algorithm? How does your answer change when the AI is deployed by a government vs. a private company?
